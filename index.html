    HIV can only be transmitted through specific bodily fluids:

    Transmission Methods

    • Unprotected sexual contact
    • Sharing needles/syringes
    • Mother-to-child during birth/breastfeeding
    • Blood transfusions (rare with modern screening)

    Cannot Spread Through

    Casual contact, saliva, tears, sweat, or insect bites

    Effective prevention strategies include:

    Proven Methods

    • Consistent condom use
    • Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)
    • Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)
    • Sterile needle programs
    • Regular testing and partner communication

    Progress

    Mother-to-child transmission rates below 1% with proper treatment

    Modern HIV treatment enables normal life expectancy:

    Antiretroviral Therapy (ART)

    • Daily medication suppresses viral load
    • Prevents progression to AIDS
    • Reduces transmission risk by 99% (U=U)

    HIV/AIDS FAQs

    Common questions about transmission, prevention, and living with HIV

    How is HIV transmitted?

    HIV spreads through specific bodily fluids:
    - Blood
    - Semen
    - Vaginal fluids
    - Rectal fluids
    - Breast milk
    Common transmission routes include unprotected sex, sharing needles, and mother-to-child during birth/breastfeeding.

    Can HIV be prevented?

    Yes! Effective prevention methods include:
    - Using condoms consistently
    - PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis)
    - PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis)
    - Regular testing
    - Undetectable = Untransmittable (U=U) treatment
    - Sterile needle programs

    Can I get HIV from casual contact?

    No. HIV cannot be transmitted through:
    - Hugging or shaking hands
    - Sharing food/drinks
    - Toilet seats
    - Mosquito bites
    - Saliva, tears, or sweat
    Normal social contact is completely safe.

    How often should I get tested?

    Testing frequency depends on risk factors:
    - Sexually active adults: At least once yearly
    - Multiple partners/condomless sex: Every 3-6 months
    - PEP/PrEP users: Every 3 months
    - Pregnant women: Early prenatal testing
    Immediate testing if exposure suspected

    Is HIV still fatal?

    With modern treatment:
    - People with HIV can live normal lifespans
    - ART (antiretroviral therapy) suppresses the virus
    - 99% reduction in transmission risk when undetectable
    - Early diagnosis is crucial
    While not curable yet, HIV is a manageable chronic condition

    Pre-exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)

    PrEP is a daily pill that can prevent HIV infection in people who are at high risk. When taken as prescribed, PrEP reduces the risk of getting HIV from sex by about 99%.

    Learn More About PrEP

    Post-exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

    PEP is an emergency medication taken within 72 hours after possible exposure to HIV. It must be taken for 28 days to be effective in preventing HIV infection.

    Learn More About PEP
    Condoms are highly effective in preventing HIV and other STIs when used correctly.

    Medication Adherence

    Understanding the importance of consistent HIV medication adherence and strategies for success

    Modern HIV treatment is simpler: as little as 1 pill per day, compared to 20+ pills in the 1990s.

    Benefits of Consistent Adherence

    • Suppresses viral load to undetectable levels
    • Prevents drug resistance development
    • Improves quality of life and life expectancy
    • Prevents HIV transmission (U=U)
    • Reduces risk of opportunistic infections

    Consequences of Non-Adherence

    • Viral rebound and increased infectiousness
    • Development of drug resistance
    • Weakened immune system
    • Progression to AIDS
    • More complex treatment regimens

    Strategies for Successful Adherence

    Set Daily Reminders

    Use alarms, smartphone apps, or pillboxes with timers

    Link to Daily Routines

    Take medication with regular activities like meals

    Use Pill Organizers

    Weekly or monthly pillboxes help track doses

    Simplify Regimen

    Ask about once-daily or single-tablet options

    Build Support System

    Share treatment plan with trusted friends or family

    Regular Check-ups

    Stay in contact with healthcare providers
